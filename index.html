Wisconsin Autonomous

President

Wisconsin Autonomous (WA) is a student lead organization focused on furthering the development and research of autonomous vehicles on the UW campus. At the time of writing, WA is working to compete in two international level competitions: the Indy Autonomous Challange and the evGrand Prix. As the President, I lead a group of 30 undergraduate students, allocating responsibilities and supporting continued development. I oversee the development of all control elements and consult on vehicle progress. I also coordinate support for sponsors and facilitate outreach. Most of my time was spent integrating path planning and controls based algorithms within our Indy Autonomous Challenge control stack. As an undergraduate researcher in the Simulation-Based Engineering Lab, I created a bridge between a physics based simulation engine and our autonomous framework. This allowed our members to test the algorithms independent of physical hardware. Using simulated sensor data and the popular robotics open source software known as Robot Operating System (ROS), we successsfully and accurately demonstrated our implemented policies prior to testing on the full scale vehicle. Similarly, I designed and created an autonomous scale vehicle to be used as a test bed for control strategies. These test platforms were pivotal in the success of our team and faciliated considerable progress for our team members.

Simulation Based Engineering Lab

Undergraduate Researcher

The Simulation Based Engineering Laboratory (SBEL) is a lab focused on solving complex, real-world problems with the aid of simulation. Located in the Mechanical Engineering Building at UW, SBEL is well established and has countless publications and conference submissions around the world. As a major contributer to the open source physics engine named ProjectChrono, their research impacts a wide assortment of disciplines. As an undergraduate researcher, I first began by developing an API that bridges the popular robotics open source software known as Robot Operating System (ROS) with ProjectChrono. I then began work on a scalable, multi-agent simulation framework for autonomous vehicles known as SynChrono. Utilizing SynChrono and the Python port of ProjectChrono called PyChrono, I aided in the implementation of various control strategies to pilot a vehicle through rugged, off-road environments. Reinforcement learning was used extensively as a means to create an end-to-end control policy. Through my research, I learned inexpensible skills, such as C++, ROS and strong technical writing skills. I worked collaboratively with other graduate and undergraduate students, gaining insightful knowledge about the professional workplace. The challenging nature of SBEL provided motivation and allowed me to grow as a leader and a student.

                        ProjectChrono - Reinforcement Learning

                        Autonomous VehiclesMachine LearningProjectChrono

                        This project was initially proposed within the Simulation-Based Engineering Lab (SBEL). The goal of the project was to develop controls algorithms for autonomous vehicles. Using the Vehicle module within ProjectChrono, a highly dynamic simulation environment, a multi-vehicle scenario was created and a nueral net using TensorFlow was trained to control said vehicle. Initially, a PPO was utilized and a simple reward function and environment was used: the vehicle was rewarded for how close it got to a certain location while remaining close to a specified path and the states were simply the vehicles velocity, position and path coordinates. The process slowly became more complicated, implementing simulated sensors, such as camera's, LiDAR's and gps/imu's.
